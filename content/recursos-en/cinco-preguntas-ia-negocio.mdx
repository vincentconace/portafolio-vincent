## The mistake everyone makes

> "I'm going to get into AI. Should I buy the $1500 Mac Mini? Which tool is the best?"

**You're starting from the wrong end.** Equipment and tools are 5% of the problem. The other 95% is **clarity about your own business** — what produces results, what can be automated without losing quality, what you should eliminate before "optimizing".

Whoever starts with the tools ends up with an expensive AI suite solving problems that weren't the real ones.

These 5 questions get the order right. Answer them first. Then you decide on tools.

---

## Question 1 — What gives you the result?

### Why it matters

Most people don't know **which specific activities generate the results** in their business. They have intuition ("selling is important") but not granularity ("the first call with a new client is what predicts whether they close or not").

Without that granularity, **you automate the wrong thing**. You ask AI to write welcome emails when what actually mattered was the first call.

### Prompt

```
You're a senior business consultant. I'm giving you info about my business.

INFO:
- Industry: [...]
- Type of client: [...]
- Average ticket: [...]
- Typical sales cycle: [...]
- Team: [N people doing what]

QUESTION:
Of EVERYTHING we do in a typical week, which are the 3-5
activities that MOST impact closing a sale / retaining a client?

RULES:
- Don't tell me "marketing" or "sales" (too generic)
- Do tell me specific activities: "first demo with client",
  "day-3 follow-up", "answer a price objection within 1h"
- If you can't know it without more info, tell me WHAT to ask you
```

What comes out might surprise you. And what comes out **is what you do NOT want to automate 100%** — you want to improve it, scale it, not replace it.

## Question 2 — Where is it written down?

### Why it matters

Everything you know about your business — but that isn't written down — **can't be shared, scaled, or automated**. It lives in your head.

If you want AI to help you, first **you need the info to be somewhere the AI can read it**. It doesn't have to be perfect. But it has to exist.

### Audit

List what you have documented and what you don't:

| Question | Does it exist? | Where |
|----------|----------------|-------|
| Who your ideal client is | | |
| What clients who are **not** a good fit look like | | |
| Your sales process step by step | | |
| The most common objections and how to answer them | | |
| Your prices and when they vary | | |
| Your brand tone / how to write like your company | | |
| Your main competitors and differentiators | | |

**If more than 4 say "doesn't exist / in my head"**: that's your first job. Before AI, **write it down**.

### Prompt to speed it up

```
I need to document [X aspect of my business]. Interview me in a
structured way — precise questions that force me to be specific —
and at the end compile the answers into a usable doc.

ASPECT TO DOCUMENT: [e.g. "my ideal client"]

Ask me 8-12 questions. Not generic ones. One per message. Wait for my
answer before the next one. At the end, compile a structured doc.
```

You spend 30 min answering. You end up with a real doc that's useful for endless things afterward.

## Question 3 — What shouldn't exist anymore?

### Why it matters

**Before optimizing something, consider eliminating it.** A lot of people automate tasks that shouldn't be done at all.

Classic examples:

- Weekly reports nobody reads → automating them solves nothing. **Cancel them.**
- Recurring meetings with little value → automating the agenda doesn't save them. **Eliminate them.**
- Bureaucratic processes that exist "because that's how it's always been" → automating bureaucracy is **automated bureaucracy**, still bureaucracy.

### Prompt

```
I'm giving you a list of recurring tasks/processes in my business:

[list]

For each one, evaluate:
1. Does it generate direct value or is it overhead?
2. If we eliminate it today, what breaks?
3. If we eliminate it, what's needed so that NOBODY MISSES IT?

Order the processes from "easiest to eliminate" to "necessary, needs
to be optimized".

RULES:
- Be brutal, not diplomatic
- If something is overhead in disguise, say so
- Don't suggest I "optimize" what can be eliminated
```

Sometimes it turns out **40-60% of your processes are unnecessary**. Eliminating beats automating 10×.

## Question 4 — What's the simplest version?

### Why it matters

People want "agents that do everything". The mistake: high complexity before proving the small version works.

**Start with the MVP of the MVP.** The agent that does **one single thing** and does it well. Then you scale.

### Examples

❌ "I want an agent that manages my entire business"
✅ "I want an agent that every morning reads my unanswered emails and drafts a reply for the 3 most urgent ones"

❌ "I want AI that automates sales"
✅ "I want a prompt that, when a new lead comes in, enriches it with public info and classifies it as hot/warm/cold"

### Prompt to decide the MVP

```
I have this big vision of what I want to do with AI in my business:

[big vision]

Break that down into the smallest possible MVP:
- What's the version that can be built in 1-2 hours?
- What does it explicitly leave out?
- How do I measure whether it works?
- If it works, what's the next block (not everything, the NEXT one)?

RULES:
- If you propose something that takes more than 4h to build, it's not an MVP
- If it requires data I don't have ready, it's not an MVP
- If it depends on coordinating 3 systems, it's not an MVP
```

The MVP that comes out is **the one you're going to build this week**. The big vision stays as a roadmap, not as an immediate deliverable.

## Question 5 — What's still on you to do?

### Why it matters

People underestimate what **you** still have to do even with AI. They end up thinking AI is "hands-free" and get disappointed.

**AI is leverage, not autopilot.** Knowing what's on you saves you from badly set expectations.

### The things that are NOT delegated to AI

| Area | What stays yours |
|------|------------------|
| **Strategic vision** | Deciding where the business is headed |
| **Critical decisions** | Hiring, firing, pivoting |
| **Key relationships** | Calls with top clients, meetings with partners |
| **Judgment** | Knowing when AI output is good or garbage |
| **Validation with real humans** | Talking to clients, not extrapolating from data |
| **Learning the craft** | Your skill growing, not atrophying |

### Prompt to clarify it

```
I'm implementing AI for [X areas] of my business. Before I do it,
I want to clarify what things I SHOULD KEEP DOING MYSELF.

Give me an honest list of:
1. The decisions I should NOT delegate to AI (with reasons)
2. The activities where AI does 80% but the 20% is critical that it be me
3. The skills I'll keep needing manually (the ones I'll lose
   if I don't practice them)
4. The risks of delegating too much

RULE: don't tell me "everything needs human supervision" (obvious).
Be specific about WHAT supervision and where.
```

## The tools-first trap

After the 5 questions, you can decide on tools with real criteria:

- What result do you want to move? (question 1)
- What info do you already have written down? (question 2)
- What to eliminate before optimizing? (question 3)
- What's the MVP of the solution? (question 4)
- What do you have to keep doing? (question 5)

**With those answers, choosing a tool is trivial.** Without those answers, whatever tool you pick is likely the wrong one.

---

## The #1 mistake people make

Skipping the questions and buying the Mac Mini.

3 months later: expensive machine, multiple subscriptions, random agents configured, and the business in the same place. Because the problem wasn't the processing power — it was the lack of clarity.

**The 5 questions can be answered in an afternoon.** After that, the rest is informed execution.
