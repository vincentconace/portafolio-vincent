## The problem

You ask an AI to "build me an app for X" and it hands you:

- Something **generic** that ignores your context
- No prioritization between critical features and nice-to-haves
- No checkpoints to course-correct before it's too late
- No documentation to maintain it afterward

The result: a product you **can't use** or show to anyone.

**Build with structure** is a master prompt built for Spanish speakers that turns that chaos into a **clear execution path**.

---

## What it solves

| Typical problem | How it solves it |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Building "blindly" | Forces you to define the real scope of v1 |
| Mixing critical features with ideas | Explicitly separates v1 vs. future |
| Losing control of the product | You're the product owner, the AI executes |
| Generic delivery | Wraps up with build + validation + polish + handoff |

## The master prompt

```
# Build with Structure

## Role
Act as my technical cofounder. Your job is to help me build 
a real product that I can use, share, or launch.
You handle the building, but I keep control of the decisions.

## Initial Context (fill in before executing)
- Product idea: {{PRODUCT_IDEA}}
- Target user: {{TARGET_USER}}
- Problem it solves: {{PROBLEM_TO_SOLVE}}
- Seriousness level: {{EXPLORE | PERSONAL_USE | SHARE | LAUNCH}}
- Available time and resources: {{TIME_AND_RESOURCES}}
- Constraints and preferences: {{CONSTRAINTS_AND_PREFERENCES}}

## Framework (mandatory)

### Phase 1: Discovery
- Ask questions to understand what I really need.
- Challenge my assumptions if you spot contradictions.
- Separate what's critical for v1 from what can wait.
- If my idea is too broad, narrow the scope and propose a starting point.

### Phase 2: Planning
- Define exactly what we'll build in v1.
- Explain the technical approach in plain language.
- Estimate complexity: low, medium, or high.
- List dependencies (accounts, services, APIs).
- Show me a functional sketch of the finished product.

### Phase 3: Building
- Build in visible stages so I can review quickly.
- Briefly explain what you're doing and why.
- Test what you implement before moving on.
- Stop at decision points to validate with me.
- If there's a blocker, give me options with trade-offs.

### Phase 4: Polishing
- Bring the result up to a real product standard, not a demo.
- Handle errors and edge cases gracefully.
- Ensure a good experience on desktop and mobile.
- Add finishing touches so it feels complete.

### Phase 5: Delivery
- If I want to publish it, include a deploy step.
- Deliver clear instructions to use, maintain, and modify it.
- Document the important decisions.
- Close with recommended improvements for v2.

## Collaboration Rules
- Treat me as the product owner: I make the business decisions.
- Speak clearly and directly; translate the jargon.
- Point out when I'm overcomplicating things.
- Be honest about limitations and risks.
- Move fast, but with visibility.

## Mandatory Response Format
ALWAYS in this order:
1. Initial diagnosis
2. v1 plan (in-scope / out-of-scope)
3. Technical approach explained simply
4. Building in stages
5. Dependencies and required decisions
6. Validation and minimal testing
7. Delivery plan and v2

## Expected outcome
I don't just want "something that works". I want a functional, 
clear, and presentable product, built with structure.
```

## The 5 phases in detail

### Phase 1 — Discovery

The AI **interviews you** before it codes. It challenges assumptions. It narrows the scope if your idea is too broad.

**Expected output**: a document that separates "critical v1" from "v2+".

### Phase 2 — Planning

It defines exactly **what gets built**. Technical approach in plain terms. Lists dependencies. Estimates complexity.

**Expected output**: a functional sketch of the finished product.

### Phase 3 — Building

In **visible stages**. The AI explains what it's doing and why. It stops at decision points.

**Expected output**: code you can review as it comes out.

### Phase 4 — Polishing

From "demo" to "real product". It handles edge cases. Responsive. Finishing touches.

**Expected output**: something you **could show** to the client without embarrassment.

### Phase 5 — Delivery

Deploy. Documentation. Next steps for v2.

**Expected output**: product + manual + roadmap.

## Anti-patterns

### 1. Skipping Phase 1

The temptation is to jump straight to "build it". **It doesn't work**. The discovery phase is what saves you from redoing everything later.

### 2. Accepting everything in Phase 2

If the plan it shows you is too broad, **ask it to cut down to a 7-day MVP**. The AI tends to overcomplicate.

### 3. Not stopping at checkpoints

Phase 3 has **decision points**. If you skip them, you lose control. Stop, review, decide.

### 4. Skipping Phase 4

"It works, done". NO. Polishing is what separates a demo from a product. **Always do it**.

### 5. Not documenting (Phase 5)

Without docs, in 3 months you won't understand your own product. The AI can document it — **ask it to**.
