## What changed with Andromeda

Andromeda is the recommendation engine Meta uses to decide who to show each ad to. You used to be able to compensate for a weak creative with a good interest stack. Now **the creative is the targeting**: Meta reads your copy, your video's first frame, your landing page, and decides distribution based on that.

**What this looks like in practice**: accounts with a system scale well. Accounts without a system run in circles: they launch 40 ads with no method, one eats the budget, the others get shut off, and nobody knows what made the winner win.

This guide sums up **8 operational rules** and prompts so the creative is built with a system, not with intuition.

---

## The 3 core ideas

Before the rules, the principles that run through all of them:

1. **The creative is the targeting** — what you say in the ad defines who Meta goes out to find
2. **Your landing page is targeting too** — if the creative says one thing and the landing says another, Meta notices and lowers distribution
3. **The first frame matters** — the first 3 seconds define whether the algorithm keeps showing it

Everything that follows is about operationalizing this.

## Rule 1 — Mine the customer's real language

The typical mistake: writing copy out of your own head, using words **you** use (industry jargon, technical benefits).

The customer doesn't search with those words. You have to mine the customer's **real language** from 4 sources:

| Source | What it gives you |
|--------|----------|
| **Reviews of your product / competitor** | How they describe the problem and the solution |
| **Reddit / niche forums** | Natural language with no marketing filter |
| **Comments on competitor ads** | Objections, doubts, desires |
| **Sales / support calls** (transcript) | Exactly how your real customers say it |

### Prompt to mine language

```
You're a copy analyst. I'll give you raw text (reviews, comments, transcripts).
I need you to extract the customer's real language.

TEXTS:
[paste all the raw material]

RETURN:

## Standout verbatim phrases
[10 EXACT customer phrases worth reusing — don't paraphrase]

## Pain points in their words
[5 pains named in THEIR words, not yours]

## Benefits they highlight
[5 benefits they mention, ordered by frequency]

## Words they do NOT use
[your/marketing words they never mention]

RULES:
- Verbatim phrases, not summaries in your words
- If a phrase appears several times, flag it
- If you detect an objection pattern, list it separately
```

## Rule 2 — Map your audience's awareness

Not everyone in your audience is at the same level of awareness. The 5 classic levels (Eugene Schwartz, still valid):

| Level | What the customer knows | How you talk to them |
|-------|---------------------|----------------|
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know they have the problem | Tell them the problem first |
| **Problem aware** | Knows they have the problem, doesn't know there's a solution | Show them there's a solution |
| **Solution aware** | Knows there's a solution, doesn't know your product | Show them your specific solution |
| **Product aware** | Knows your product, isn't buying yet | Remove objections, show proof |
| **Most aware** | Ready to buy | Offer + urgency |

**The gap that caps your spend ceiling**: most accounts only have ads for 1-2 levels. Missing levels → missing audience to scale into.

### Prompt to map awareness

```
I have my customer avatar: [detailed avatar description].

My product solves: [problem].

Distribute my target audience across the 5 awareness levels:

## Estimated distribution
[% at each level with justification]

## For each level, what an ad would need to say
[core message + type of proof + CTA for each level]

## The level where I currently have the fewest ads (assume that I do)
[which one and why it's probably that way]
```

## Rule 3 — Brief with an explicit first frame

Your production brief must include **exactly** what's shown in the first second. Not "dynamic brand video." Something concrete:

❌ "Strong hook that grabs attention"
✅ "First frame: person spinning quickly toward the camera with an 'I got robbed' look. Big subtitle: 'I got charged 3 times in the same month.' No brand logo in the first 3 seconds."

Meta reads the first frame visually. If the first second is generic (logo fading in, a landscape), the algorithm has no signal to classify the ad.

### Prompt for a creative brief

```
You're a creative director. I need a production brief for a video ad for
[product] aimed at awareness level [unaware/problem aware/etc].

CUSTOMER CONTEXT:
- Avatar: [description]
- Main pain point in their words: [verbatim phrase from mining]
- Awareness level: [level]

RETURN:

## Hook (first 3 seconds)
- Exact frame shown at second 0 (concrete visual description)
- Subtitle/copy that appears
- Audio (if any)

## Development (seconds 3-12)
[problem narrative in the customer's language]

## Solution (seconds 12-25)
[how the product shows up without looking spammy]

## Close (last 5 seconds)
[CTA + final visual]

## Notes for the editor
- Aspect ratio: 9:16 for reels / 1:1 for feed
- Burned-in subtitles: yes (90% watch without audio)
- Logo: NOT in the first 3 seconds
- Music: [type / reference]

RULES:
- First frame must be visually specific, not generic
- The hook does NOT mention the brand
- The hook copy must use mined language, not jargon
```

## Rule 4 — Landing as part of the creative

You used to be able to send all your traffic to a generic landing page. Now Meta reads the landing and compares it to the ad. If they don't match, it lowers distribution.

**A pattern that works**:

1. **One landing page per ad angle**, not a generic landing
2. The landing's **headline** should match the ad's hook (not identical — but the same message)
3. The **first section** validates that the customer is in the right place
4. **Then** the product pitch comes in

### Prompt to audit a landing page

```
You're a UX writer. Audit this landing against the ad that brings people to it.

AD:
- Hook: [hook]
- Main promise: [promise]
- Customer language: [mined phrases]

LANDING (URL or copy):
[content]

REVIEW:

## Ad-landing match
- Does the landing headline validate the ad's promise? (yes/no + quote)
- Is there language continuity? (yes/no + examples)
- Does a user landing here from the ad know they're in the right place within 3 seconds?

## Issues found
[list with file:line / section if applicable]

## Concrete suggested changes
[action by action, with before/after text]
```

## Rule 5 — Decision framework BEFORE launching

When the dashboard is on fire (one ad blows up, another dies), you decide emotionally. **That burns budget.** The fix: define your decision framework **before** launching, not during.

### Variables I define beforehand

- **Fixed budget per ad**: how much each ad is allowed to lose before killing it
- **Key success metric**: CPA, ROAS, hookrate — ONE, not 5
- **Pause threshold**: at what number an ad gets killed (e.g., CPA > 1.5x your target for 48h with +$X spent)
- **Scale threshold**: at what number you double the budget (e.g., ROAS > 2x target for 72h)

Write it in a shared doc. When it's time to decide, you consult it. No negotiation.

## Rule 6 — `ultrathink` before the big decisions

A small trick for Claude: when you hand it a complex analysis (account audit, deciding which creative to scale), add the word **`ultrathink`** to the prompt.

It works like explicitly asking it to **think harder before answering**. For analytical tasks in Meta Ads, the difference is noticeable.

```
ultrathink

I have these 8 ads running for 14 days. [data]

Which should I scale, which should I pause, which should I leave alone?

Justify each decision with the specific metric that backs it.
Do NOT use averages — look at the outliers.
```

## Rule 7 — Iterate on variables, not on ideas

The typical mistake: launching 5 "very different" ads thinking it's testing. Since each one changes 5 variables at once, you can't learn anything from the result.

**What DOES work**: isolated variables.

| Test | Single variable that changes |
|------|----------------------------|
| Test A vs B | First-frame hook (everything else the same) |
| Test C vs D | CTA at the end (everything else the same) |
| Test E vs F | Music (everything else the same) |

You learn what moved the needle. Without this, it's all noise.

### Prompt to design tests

```
I have a creative variant that performs at [metric]. I want to test N hypotheses:

HYPOTHESES:
1. If I change the hook to [X], conversion goes up because [reason]
2. If I change the CTA to [Y], CTR goes up because [reason]
...

Design me a test plan:
- Which tests do I run first? (prioritize by expected impact)
- How many ads per test?
- Minimum budget for the result to be significant?
- When to declare a winner? (explicit criterion)
- What do I do with the losers? (kill / learn / archive)
```

## Rule 8 — Weekly audit with a system

Every Monday, audit the account with the same structure:

| Section | What you review |
|---------|-------------|
| **Hookrate** | Hooks that are working vs the ones that aren't |
| **Awareness gap** | Levels where I have no ads |
| **Frequency** | Burned-out audiences (frequency >3) |
| **Test pipeline** | Which tests are running, which closed, conclusions |
| **Next creatives** | 3 concepts to produce this week based on what you learned |

Turn this into a slash command (like the [9 prompts for Cowork](/boveda/nueve-prompts-cowork)) that runs automatically every Monday and delivers the report.

## Anti-patterns

### 1. Expecting Claude to "make the ad" with no context

Don't tell it "build me ads for my product X." Without mined language + mapped awareness + a structured brief, it'll invent generic stuff that doesn't convert.

### 2. Changing 5 variables at once

If your test doesn't isolate the variable, you learn nothing. Test one variable at a time, or don't test.

### 3. Clinging to a creative "because you love it"

A creative you love but that performs poorly is performing poorly. The account won't wait for the algorithm to "get your vision." Kill the creative, keep the learning.

### 4. Outsourcing without a system

If your agency/creator doesn't know this, they'll hand you 5 "very creative" ads that don't get distributed. Give them **the brief with the explicit first frame** and the mined language. The result improves.
