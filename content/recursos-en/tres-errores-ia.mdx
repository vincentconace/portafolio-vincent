## The core idea

> Zero context, dumb AI. Full context, monster AI.

The 3 mistakes we all make with AI — **and why all 3 are fixed by the same rule**.

---

## The analogy

Imagine you swap cars every time you show up late to work. You bought 3 cars, changed 4 engines, **you're still showing up late**.

At some point you realize: **the problem wasn't the car**.

It's the same with AI:
- You switch models
- You pay for another plan
- You try another tool
- The answer stays generic

**The problem travels with you**. It's the driver, not the car.

---

## The 3 mistakes

### Mistake 1 — You blame the AI

You asked it to *"make a viral video"* and it gives you nonsense. You say the AI is **garbage**.

**The model gave you exactly what you gave it**: nothing. A viral video about what? For whom? In what tone? With no info, the model **guesses** — and guesses wrong.

### Mistake 2 — You assume the AI knows you

The AI has access to half the internet. But it **knows nothing about you**.

- Not your project
- Not your audience
- Not what you sell
- Not the tone you use

Assuming it knows you because "it's smart" = guaranteed disappointment.

### Mistake 3 — You jump between models

You ask ChatGPT for a plan, then paste it into Claude to build it out. It comes out crooked.

**Each model reads differently** and fills the gaps differently. The "tone" ChatGPT assumed, Claude won't assume the same way.

### The 3 are really one

They look like 3 different mistakes. They're the same one: **you didn't give it context**.

- You blame the model because it didn't read your mind
- You assume it knows you because some old thread once did
- You jump between models looking for one that guesses better

All 3 are the same complaint: **"the AI didn't get it"**. The AI didn't get it because **you didn't explain it**.

## The rule — 5 dimensions of context

| # | Dimension | Question |
|---|-----------|----------|
| 1 | **Role and project** | Who you are, what you're building |
| 2 | **Audience** | Who this is for |
| 3 | **What you sell or build** | The specific product/service |
| 4 | **Current situation** | Where you stand |
| 5 | **What you want to happen** | The expected outcome |

> **If you don't hit 4 of 5, the prompt is badly written.**

---

## Before and after

### Without context (all 5 mistakes)

```
Make me a viral video
```

Response: generic, for the "average creator". Useful to no one.

### With context (5 dimensions hit)

```
You're a creative director for B2B SaaS.

Me: founder of [YOUR SAAS], I sell inventory management to
mid-sized LATAM retail (role + what I sell).

Audience: COOs / ops directors at retailers with 5-50 stores
(audience).

Situation: we launched 6 months ago, $15k MRR, we want to get to
$50k MRR (current situation).

I want: 3 concepts for a 30s Reel for LinkedIn that hook this
audience, with hooks based on their real pain points (what I
want to happen).
```

Response: hyper-specific, actionable, useful.

## The mental checklist — 7 questions

Before sending an important prompt, answer:

1. Did I tell it **who I am** and what I'm building?
2. Did I tell it **who** this is for?
3. Did I tell it **where I stand** (current state)?
4. Did I tell it **what I want to see in the end** (outcome)?
5. Did I tell it **what constraints** I have?
6. Did I tell it **the format** of the output?
7. Did I tell it **what NOT to do**?

**If you hit 3 "no" answers in a row → DON'T send it**. Rewrite it.

---

## Why it's not "more words"

Someone can write a long paragraph and **still not be giving context** — they just repeat the request in longer sentences.

**Context is the right words in the right order**: who you are, who this is for, what you're building, where you stand, what you want to see in the end.

5 pieces of info. The difference between an empty prompt and a prompt that works is usually **4 sentences**.

## 3 copy-paste prompts

### Template 1 — for creative tasks

```
CONTEXT:
- I'm a [ROLE] in [INDUSTRY]
- My product/service: [DESCRIPTION IN 1 LINE]
- My audience: [SPECIFIC TARGET]
- My current situation: [STATE]
- My brand tone: [DESCRIPTION]

I WANT:
[WHAT I NEED]

CONSTRAINTS:
[WHAT NOT TO DO / LIMITATIONS]

OUTPUT FORMAT:
[HOW I WANT IT STRUCTURED]
```

### Template 2 — for technical tasks

```
TECHNICAL CONTEXT:
- Stack: [TECH]
- My level: [BEGINNER / INTERMEDIATE / ADVANCED]
- Project conventions: [RULES]
- Codebase state: [DESCRIPTION]

PROBLEM:
[WHAT I NEED]

SUCCESS CRITERIA:
[HOW I KNOW IT'S RIGHT]

WHAT NOT TO DO:
[TECHNICAL CONSTRAINTS]
```

### Template 3 — for decisions

```
SITUATION:
[WHERE I AM]

OPTIONS:
[ALTERNATIVES I KNOW OF]

FACTORS THAT MATTER TO ME:
[WHAT WEIGHS ON THE DECISION]

I'M AFRAID OF:
[PERCEIVED RISKS]

I WANT YOU TO ANALYZE:
[WHAT SPECIFICALLY]
```

## Anti-patterns

### 1. Talking to the AI like a friend

❌ "Hey, help me with this"
✅ "You're a [ROLE]. I'm [X]. I need [Y] for [PURPOSE]"

The AI isn't a friend. It's an assistant that needs a brief.

### 2. Assuming continuity between chats

Every NEW chat starts from scratch. If you trust that it "knows" because you talked before in another chat, **guaranteed disappointment**.

### 3. Switching models when something doesn't work

If it gave you a bad answer, it was probably a bad prompt. **Improve the prompt before switching models**.

### 4. Expecting the model to "read between the lines"

Models are literal. If you want something, **say it explicitly**. Hinting doesn't work.

### 5. Ignoring the output when it's vague

If the answer is generic, **it's a sign** your prompt was vague. DON'T blame the model. Re-prompt.

## When this rule does NOT help you

| Case | Why |
|------|---------|
| Super simple one-off tasks ("what time is it?") | Unnecessary overhead |
| Exploratory brainstorming | You want the AI to propose, not answer something specific |
| Learning (you want a general explanation) | Asking for context in order to learn is a paradox |

## The rule in one line

> **The problem with AI isn't the AI. It's that you didn't explain it.**

Learn this once = **it serves you forever**, no matter which model you use.
